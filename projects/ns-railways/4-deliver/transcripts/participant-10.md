# Interview Transcript -- P10

**Date:** 17 September 2025
**Duration:** 45 minutes
**Interviewer:** Daan Verbeke
**Participant:** P10
**Location:** Starbucks, Rotterdam Centraal Station
**Research context:** Evaluative research on the multimodal journey planner in the NS app

---

**Interviewer:** Thanks for agreeing to this interview. You're visiting from Germany, is that right?

**P10:** Yes, from Cologne. I'm here for five days, visiting the Netherlands for what must be the fourth or fifth time. I come here fairly regularly. I arrived on Monday, today is Wednesday, so day three. I'm doing the classic route: Amsterdam, Rotterdam, Den Haag. The triangle. I love these three cities because they're so different from each other and so easy to reach by train. It's one of the things I appreciate about the Netherlands -- everything is close together and well connected.

**Interviewer:** And how have you been getting around on this trip?

**P10:** Mostly by train, which feels very natural to me because I use the train a lot in Germany too. The train system here is quite similar to Deutsche Bahn in some ways -- Intercity trains between major cities, regional trains for shorter distances -- but honestly, NS is more reliable. The trains are more frequent and they're more punctual. Not perfect, but better than what I'm used to at home.

I bought a tourist day pass on the first day, which was convenient, and then from day two I switched to single tickets through the NS app because it gave me more flexibility. I like being able to decide spontaneously where to go without worrying about whether my day pass covers a certain area.

**Interviewer:** So you're using the NS app. How did you find out about it?

**P10:** I downloaded it before my trip. I always do a bit of research before traveling, it's the German in me I suppose. I was reading a travel blog, an English-language blog about visiting the Netherlands, and they had a section on transport apps. They recommended the NS app specifically and mentioned that you can now plan multimodal trips, with trains and buses and trams all together. That caught my attention because I know from experience that getting from a train station to your actual destination can be the most confusing part of traveling. The train is easy. The last kilometer is hard.

**Interviewer:** That's interesting. So you found out about the journey planner feature through a blog, not through the app itself?

**P10:** Exactly. And I think that says something important. If I hadn't read that specific blog post, I probably would have downloaded the app, used it to buy tickets, and never discovered the journey planner. Because when you first open the app, what do you see? A big prominent button for buying tickets, which makes sense, that's the primary action. And then there's a lot of other things -- your travel history, your account, some promotions. The Reisplanner is there somewhere, but it doesn't stand out as something special. It looks like a standard departure board feature.

I wouldn't have guessed that behind that button is a full multimodal journey planner that can combine trains, trams, buses, and walking into one integrated view. That's a sophisticated feature hidden behind an ordinary-looking button with a Dutch name. For a tourist who doesn't speak Dutch and isn't specifically looking for it, it's essentially invisible.

**Interviewer:** And you've been using the journey planner during your trip?

**P10:** Yes, several times now, and I have to say, my experience has been mostly positive. The first time was on Monday afternoon. I needed to get from Amsterdam Centraal to my hotel, which is near Vondelpark, not right next to any train station. I opened the journey planner, typed in the hotel address, and it gave me a route: tram 2 from Centraal Station, seven stops, then a four-minute walk. It showed me the tram number, which stop to get off at, the expected arrival time, and how far to walk from there. That was very smooth and very reassuring for a first-time experience.

**Interviewer:** So a positive first impression?

**P10:** Yes, definitely. And then I used it again that evening to go from the hotel to a restaurant near the Jordaan. That was just walking, fifteen minutes, and the app showed me the route. Simple enough. The next day I used it for something more substantial -- the train from Amsterdam to Rotterdam. And that's where the app really shone. It showed me the Intercity train, the platform number, and crucially, that the train had one minute of delay. One minute! That level of precision is impressive. In Germany, DB Navigator sometimes doesn't update the delay until the train is already five minutes late. Here it was proactive.

And during the train ride, the app updated in real time. It showed me that despite the one-minute delay, my journey time would be as planned because the train would make up the time. That kind of proactive, contextual information is really valuable. It reduces the mental load of traveling.

**Interviewer:** Where did you run into difficulties, if any?

**P10:** The first day, at Amsterdam Centraal. Despite having the app and having planned the tram route, I still got a bit lost at the station. Amsterdam Centraal is a large and confusing station, especially if you're arriving from outside. There are multiple levels, multiple exits, tram stops on different sides of the station, and the signage inside isn't always clear about which exit leads to which tram.

The app told me to take tram 2, and it showed "walk to tram stop," but it didn't tell me which exit to take. And Amsterdam Centraal has -- what, five or six exits? I took the wrong one, ended up on the side facing the IJ river, where there are ferries but no tram 2. I had to walk around the station, which added maybe eight minutes to my journey. Not a disaster, but not a great experience either, especially with a suitcase after a train ride.

**Interviewer:** How did you solve it?

**P10:** Google Maps. I opened Google Maps for the walking navigation, because it shows you the blue dot that moves with you and the compass that rotates. Within thirty seconds I could see that I needed to go to the other side of the station. The NS app had the right tram, the right time, the right stop -- all the intellectual information was correct. But it couldn't physically guide me to the tram stop. That's the missing piece. The navigation from point A to point B within a station or a city.

And this is a pattern I've noticed across my trip. The NS app excels at what I'd call "information" -- schedules, platforms, delays, connections, pricing. It knows everything about the transit system. But it's weak on "navigation" -- actually guiding you through physical space. Where to walk, which exit to take, which direction to turn. Google Maps is the opposite: weaker on transit information but excellent on navigation.

**Interviewer:** That's a very clear analysis. Let's explore the app together. I'll plan a trip from Rotterdam to the Vermeer Centrum in Delft.

**P10:** Oh, that's on my list for tomorrow. Let me see what the app says. So, train from Rotterdam Centraal to Delft, seven minutes. Intercity. Platform 2. And then walk ten minutes to the Vermeer Centrum. That's very straightforward. I see the next train is in four minutes. I could literally leave right now, walk to platform 2, and be at the Vermeer Centrum in twenty minutes. This kind of spontaneity is wonderful for a tourist. You can just decide to go somewhere and the app tells you it's twenty minutes away and the next train is in four minutes. That's powerful.

**Interviewer:** Now let's try something more complex. An address in Gouda, not at the station, somewhere in the old town.

**P10:** Okay, I type the address. Train to Gouda, and then... fourteen minutes walking. That's quite a lot of walking. Is there no bus option? Let me look... The app only shows the walking route. Hmm. Let me check Google Maps for comparison... Google Maps also suggests train plus walking. So maybe there really isn't a convenient bus for this particular address. Fair enough.

But here's where the difference shows. The walking route on the NS app is a blue line on a map, zoomed fairly far out. I can see the general direction but not the specific streets. On Google Maps, the walking route is a detailed path with street names, turn-by-turn instructions, and an estimated time that updates as you walk. If I were actually doing this walk in Gouda, a city I don't know, I would absolutely switch to Google Maps for the walking portion.

**Interviewer:** And a more complex multimodal route? Let's try Rotterdam to Kinderdijk.

**P10:** Kinderdijk! Yes, the windmills. That's actually why I came to Rotterdam, partly. Let me plan it. Train to Dordrecht, and then... oh, this is interesting. It shows a waterbus option. Waterbus 202 from Dordrecht to Kinderdijk. That's charming! I didn't know about the waterbus. Total time about fifty minutes. And I can see the departure time for the waterbus, which is twenty minutes after the train arrives. That's a comfortable connection, not too tight.

Let me check Google Maps for the same trip. Hmm, Google Maps suggests a completely different route. It suggests taking a bus, not the waterbus. The bus route is slightly faster but much less scenic. The NS app found the waterbus, which is clearly the more interesting option for a tourist. That's a genuine advantage -- the NS app knows about local Dutch transport options that Google Maps might not prioritize.

**Interviewer:** So the NS app found something Google Maps missed?

**P10:** Or at least deprioritized. I think Google Maps probably knows about the waterbus, but it optimizes for speed, and the bus is faster. The NS app seems to integrate better with Dutch public transport, including these more unusual options like waterbuses and maybe ferries. For a tourist, the scenic option is often the better option. We're not in a hurry, we're here to experience things. A waterbus ride to the windmills is part of the experience, not just a means of transport.

**Interviewer:** How would you compare the NS app and Google Maps overall for your trip?

**P10:** They complement each other. The NS app is superior for transit information: platform numbers, real-time delays, connections, ticket purchases, integration with Dutch public transport including waterbuses and local lines. Google Maps is superior for navigation: walking directions, orienting yourself in a city, understanding where you are in relation to where you need to be.

If I had to choose only one, and I was forced to choose, I would probably choose Google Maps. Because it does everything adequately. Not brilliantly for trains, but adequately. The NS app, on the other hand, does trains brilliantly but doesn't do walking navigation at all. In practical terms, I need walking navigation more often than I need precise platform numbers.

But with both apps, my experience is significantly better than with either alone. The NS app tells me what I need to know, Google Maps tells me where I need to go. Together they cover everything. The question is: could the NS app do everything so well that I don't need Google Maps? Right now, no. But it's closer than I expected when I started this trip.

**Interviewer:** What's the main gap that prevents the NS app from being a standalone solution?

**P10:** Walking navigation, without question. The NS app shows me a line on a map for walking sections. Google Maps shows me street-by-street directions with a compass, distance markers, and a position indicator that follows me in real time. For a tourist in an unfamiliar city, that difference is the difference between confidence and anxiety.

When I'm standing at Rotterdam Centraal and the app says "walk twelve minutes to your hotel," my first question is: which direction do I start walking? The NS app gives me a line on a map that I have to interpret. Google Maps gives me an arrow that says "walk west on Kruisplein." One requires spatial reasoning, the other requires following an arrow. For a tired tourist who just got off a train, the arrow wins every time.

**Interviewer:** And beyond walking navigation?

**P10:** Discoverability. I found the journey planner because I read a travel blog. That is a very specific and unlikely path. My friend who is visiting Amsterdam next month -- she doesn't read travel blogs. She will download the NS app because someone tells her to, she will buy a ticket, and she will never discover the journey planner. And she's exactly the person who needs it most. A tourist who doesn't know the Dutch transit system, who doesn't speak Dutch, who would benefit enormously from having all her transit options in one view.

The feature should be front and center for new users. Not buried behind a Dutch label in a crowded interface. Maybe a short onboarding flow when you first open the app: "Are you visiting the Netherlands? Here's how to plan your trip." Two screens, thirty seconds. That would make a world of difference.

**Interviewer:** Are there other improvements you'd suggest specifically for tourists?

**P10:** Yes, I have a small list, actually. I've been thinking about this during my trip.

First, the language switching should be automatic. My phone is set to English, the app should detect this and present itself in English. This is standard practice in international apps. The fact that the NS app doesn't do this means that every non-Dutch-speaking tourist sees a Dutch interface by default, which is immediately alienating.

Second, practical information for tourists embedded in the journey results. When the app shows me a route that includes a bus, it should tell me: "You can use your OV-chipkaart on this bus. Remember to check in when boarding." Or when it shows a waterbus: "This waterbus is included in your day pass" or "This waterbus costs an additional 4 euros." Right now, I don't know what's included in what. The pricing is opaque for tourists.

Third, show me the total cost of the entire journey upfront. Not just the train ticket, but the whole thing. If I'm going from Rotterdam to Kinderdijk and it involves a train and a waterbus, show me one price for the whole trip. Don't make me wonder if the waterbus is extra.

And fourth -- this is more of a nice-to-have -- tourist-relevant information at destinations. When I plan a trip to Kinderdijk, maybe show me the opening hours. Or when I plan a trip to the Mauritshuis, show me that it's closed on Mondays. This goes beyond transport planning, I know, but it would make the app genuinely indispensable for tourists.

**Interviewer:** Those are very well thought out suggestions. One final question: if you were telling a friend about the NS app, what would you say?

**P10:** I would say: download it before your trip. Change the language to English in the settings -- it won't do it automatically, unfortunately. And then use the Reisplanner -- the Journey Planner -- for all your train travel. It's better than Google Maps for trains because it gives you platform numbers, real-time delay information, and ticket purchasing all in one place. The real-time information is genuinely excellent, better than Deutsche Bahn, better than Google Maps for Dutch trains.

But I would also say: keep Google Maps installed. You'll need it for walking navigation and for orienting yourself in cities, because the NS app can't do that yet. Think of the NS app as your train expert and Google Maps as your city guide. Together they work beautifully.

And I would tell my friend to learn about the OV-chipkaart system before arriving. Check in, check out, twenty-euro deposit -- learn all of that before you land at Schiphol. Because the app doesn't explain it, the airport doesn't explain it clearly enough, and you'll waste money and stress if you figure it out the hard way. Like I almost did.

**Interviewer:** Would you say your overall experience has been positive?

**P10:** Surprisingly positive, yes. I came to the Netherlands prepared to use Google Maps for everything, because that's what I do in most countries. Italy, France, Spain -- Google Maps is the universal tool. But the NS app genuinely adds value that Google Maps doesn't have. The real-time information, the platform numbers, the connection advice, the ticket integration -- these things make train travel noticeably smoother.

Yesterday, my train from Amsterdam to Rotterdam had three minutes of delay, and the app immediately showed me that my planned connection in Rotterdam -- a tram to my hotel -- was not affected because the tram has a ten-minute frequency. That proactive "don't worry, you'll be fine" information is something Google Maps simply doesn't offer. And for a traveler, especially one who doesn't know the system well, that kind of reassurance is incredibly valuable.

I just wish the app were more accessible to tourists by default. The best features -- the journey planner, the real-time information, the multimodal routing -- shouldn't require a travel blog or a research interview to discover. They should be the first thing a tourist sees when they open the app. Because right now, the NS app is like a wonderful restaurant on a side street with no sign. The food is great, but only the locals know about it.

**Interviewer:** That's a wonderful metaphor to end on. Thank you for your time and your very thoughtful feedback.

**P10:** Thank you. I genuinely hope NS uses this kind of research to improve the experience for international visitors. The Dutch train system is excellent -- honestly, much better than what we have in Germany, and I say that as someone who loves Deutsche Bahn despite its many flaws. The app just needs to match the quality of the train system across the entire journey, not only the train part. And it needs to open its arms to tourists, not hide its best features behind Dutch labels and buried settings. The foundation is there. It just needs some finishing touches.
