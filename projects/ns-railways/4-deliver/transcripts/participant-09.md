# Interview Transcript -- P09

**Date:** 16 September 2025
**Duration:** 50 minutes
**Interviewer:** Myrthe Montijn
**Participant:** P09
**Location:** Coffee Company, Amsterdam Centraal Station
**Research context:** Evaluative research on the multimodal journey planner in the NS app

---

**Interviewer:** Thank you for meeting me today. So you're visiting the Netherlands for the first time?

**P09:** Yes, yes! First time. I arrive, eh, four days ago. I am traveling for one week, like a backpacker. I have my bag, I stay in hostels, I take the train, I go to different cities. Amsterdam, then Rotterdam, then maybe Delft or Den Haag, and then back to Amsterdam for my flight home. It is very nice here, really, the country is beautiful and the people are friendly. But also very... how you say... confusing? The transport here is confusing for me.

**Interviewer:** In what way confusing?

**P09:** Okay, so, in Spain, I am from Barcelona, we have the metro and the bus and the tram, and it is simple. You buy a card, the T-casual, you put it in the machine, the door opens, you get on, you get off. Here, there is this card, the OV-chip... chipkaart? I don't know how to say it exactly. And you have to check in when you enter and check out when you leave, and if you forget to check out, you pay a lot more money. Like, twenty euros for a trip that costs three euros. And nobody tell me this! Nobody at the airport said anything about this. I find out after two days when I check my bank account and I think: wait, why I pay sixty euros for three short trips? That cannot be right. But it was right, because I forget to check out every time.

**Interviewer:** That sounds really stressful. How did you figure it out?

**P09:** A guy in my hostel, from Australia, he has been in Netherlands for two weeks already and he explained me everything. He said: you have to check out, always, and also you have to put twenty euros on the card before you can travel, like a deposit. Nobody tells you this! In Barcelona, you buy a ticket, you use the ticket, finished. Here, there is deposit and checking in and checking out and different zones, it is very complicated for a person who arrives for the first time.

**Interviewer:** And on that first day, getting from the airport to Amsterdam, how did that go?

**P09:** Oh my God. The first day was the worst day. Really. I arrive at Schiphol, the airport, and I need to go to my hostel in Amsterdam, near Leidseplein. And I don't know which train, which platform, nothing. There are signs, yes, some in Dutch and some in English, but I am tired from the flight and there are so many people walking so fast with their suitcases, and everyone seems to know where they are going except me. I feel like I am the only person who doesn't understand anything.

So I open Google Maps, because that is what I always do in any country, and it says "take the train to Amsterdam Centraal" but it doesn't tell me which platform. It says "platform 4" actually, but when I go to the screens, the yellow screens with the departures, there are so many trains, and they are all in Dutch. "Intercity" to this place, "Sprinter" to that place. What is Intercity? What is Sprinter? I don't know these words. In Spain we have Cercanías and Renfe and AVE, that is our system. Here everything has different names.

I ask a woman with a suitcase and she is very nice, she says: take any train to Amsterdam Centraal, they all go there. And she shows me the platform. And I get on the train and I arrive in Amsterdam. But it takes me maybe thirty minutes to figure this out. Thirty minutes of stress and confusion.

**Interviewer:** And since that first day, how have you been getting around?

**P09:** Google Maps. For everything. Google Maps is my life here. I type where I want to go and it tells me: take this train, walk here, take this bus. It works okay, not perfect, but okay. Sometimes the times are not exactly right, like it says the train is at 10:15 but actually it is at 10:13 or 10:17. And it doesn't tell me the platform, so I always have to look at the yellow screen in the station. But at least it works in English and I know how to use it because I use it in Barcelona too.

The one thing I don't like about Google Maps here is the walking parts. In Amsterdam, the streets are confusing, they go in circles around the canals, and Google Maps says "walk 10 minutes" but then you walk 10 minutes and you are on the wrong side of a canal and there is no bridge. So you have to walk around. But overall, Google Maps is my tool. My only tool, really.

**Interviewer:** Have you used the NS app at all?

**P09:** The NS app? Yes, I download it because the Australian guy in the hostel, he tell me about it. He says it is cheaper to buy tickets in the app than at the machine. The machine at the station, have you used it? It is very slow. And always there is a line of ten people. And the machine is in Dutch first, you have to press a button for English, and then there are so many options. Single trip, return trip, day pass, group ticket, first class, second class -- I just want to go from here to there! In the app it is faster. So I buy my tickets in the app.

But for planning the journey, where to go, which train, no. For that I use Google Maps.

**Interviewer:** Have you noticed the journey planner feature in the NS app? The Reisplanner?

**P09:** The Reisplanner? Hmm. I see a button that says "Reisplanner" but I don't know what this word means. It is Dutch. Reis... planner? Is it like "travel planner"? Okay, I didn't know. I thought it was something... I don't know, something for Dutch people. Like a subscription or a season ticket or something. I didn't click on it because the word means nothing to me.

**Interviewer:** Let me show you. Here, in the app, you can plan a trip from one place to another, and it shows you trains, buses, trams, walking directions -- everything together in one view.

**P09:** Oh! Oh, really? Let me see. This button here? Reisplanner? And I type... where I want to go? Like an address? Okay. Oh, wow. I did not know this. This is... okay, this is nice. Why didn't I know this?

**Interviewer:** That's a really important question. Why do you think you didn't know?

**P09:** Because it is in Dutch! "Reisplanner" -- if this button said "Plan your trip" or "Journey planner" in English, I would have pressed it on day one. Guaranteed. But when I see a Dutch word that I don't understand, I skip it. I only use the parts of the app that I can understand. The ticket icon, I understand, because it looks like a ticket. The little person icon, I pressed that once and it showed my account information. But "Reisplanner" is just letters that mean nothing to me.

**Interviewer:** Can the app be switched to English?

**P09:** I don't know. Is there an option for that?

**Interviewer:** Yes, in the settings. Let me switch it for you.

**P09:** Oh, there it is. English. Okay, now it says "Journey planner." That makes more sense. But I didn't know the settings were there. In most apps I use, if my phone language is set to English, the app automatically uses English. This app didn't do that. It was in Dutch when I downloaded it. And I didn't go looking in the settings because I had already figured out how to buy tickets, so why would I explore more?

**Interviewer:** That's very useful feedback. Let's try the journey planner together now. I'll plan a trip from here, Amsterdam Centraal, to the Mauritshuis museum in Den Haag.

**P09:** Okay! This is actually where I want to go tomorrow. I have been wanting to see the Vermeer paintings. So I type Mauritshuis... it finds it! It knows the museum. Nice. And now I press "plan trip"? Okay.

Oh, wow. This is really nice. I see the train from Amsterdam to Den Haag, Intercity, 50 minutes. And then it says "walk 12 minutes" to the museum. And I see the platform -- platform 4! This is what Google Maps doesn't give me. Platform 4. Now I know exactly where to go. And it says the train is on time, with a little green indicator. Very clear. And the price, 12.40 euros, and I can buy the ticket from this screen? Directly? From the planning screen? That is very convenient.

In Google Maps, it shows me the route but then I have to go to the machine or back to the ticket section to buy the ticket. Here it is all together. Plan and buy in one step. For a tourist, this is perfect. Exactly what we need.

**Interviewer:** Would you have used this feature if you had known about it on your first day?

**P09:** One hundred percent. Absolutely. My first day, I waste maybe one hour, more maybe, trying to figure out how to go from Schiphol to Amsterdam and then from Amsterdam Centraal to my hostel. I take the wrong tram because Google Maps says "tram 2" but I don't know which direction. There are two directions, and I take the wrong one, and I end up in some neighbourhood I don't know, and I have to come back and try again. If I have this app, in English, with the platform number and the tram number and the direction and everything together, it would save me so much stress. So much.

The first day is the worst. Because you know nothing. You don't know the system, you don't know the words, you don't know the customs. And this app could help with exactly that. But I didn't know it existed. Four days in this country, using the NS app every day to buy tickets, and I never discovered the journey planner. That is a problem.

**Interviewer:** Let's try a more complex trip. Say you want to go from Amsterdam to Giethoorn, the village with no roads.

**P09:** Giethoorn! Yes, someone at the hostel told me about this place. The Venice of the Netherlands, they call it. I want to go there but I don't know how. Let me try... I type Giethoorn... it finds it! Okay, the route is: train to Steenwijk, and then bus 70 to Giethoorn. Total time two hours and something. That is a long trip but okay.

I see the train departure time, the platform, the arrival time in Steenwijk. And then the bus, bus 70, departure time, and then "walk 5 minutes" to... the village? Okay. So I take a train and then a bus. That is useful information. Without this app, how would I know to go to Steenwijk? I have never heard of Steenwijk. Google Maps would probably tell me the same, but let me check...

Yes, Google Maps gives a similar route. But Google Maps doesn't show me the platform for the train. And the NS app shows me that the train is on time, that little green indicator. I trust that more.

**Interviewer:** But are there things the NS app doesn't tell you that you'd want to know?

**P09:** Yes. The bus. How do I find the bus in Steenwijk? I don't know Steenwijk. I have never been to Steenwijk. I will arrive at the train station and I need to find bus 70. Which direction do I go when I exit the station? Left or right? Where is the bus stop? And the bus, how I pay? Can I use my OV-chipkaart? Or I need cash? Or a different ticket? The app doesn't tell me any of this.

Because this is what is stressful for a tourist. Not the big decisions. The big decision is easy: train to Steenwijk, bus to Giethoorn. I can understand that. But the small things, the practical things -- which exit, which direction to walk, where is the bus stop, how to pay for the bus, do I need to check in again on the bus -- these small things make you nervous. These are the things that make you feel stupid and lost.

Especially if you don't speak the language. Because if you are lost in Steenwijk, who do you ask? There are maybe three people at the station and they are all looking at their phones. And the announcements on the train are in Dutch. Sometimes they say something in English too, but it is very fast and my English is not perfect either, so I miss half of it. And then there are announcements that are only in Dutch and I think: is that important? Should I be worried? I don't know.

**Interviewer:** How does Google Maps handle this same trip?

**P09:** Let me compare. Google Maps gives basically the same route. Train to Steenwijk, bus to Giethoorn. But Google Maps also doesn't tell me the platform for the train. And the bus schedule -- I am not sure if it is real-time in Google Maps. The NS app has that green "on time" indicator for the train, which gives confidence. Google Maps just shows the departure time without saying if it is on time or delayed.

But Google Maps has one big advantage: the walking navigation. If I need to walk from the station to the bus stop, Google Maps shows me the direction with the blue dot that moves with me. It tells me "walk north for 100 meters, then turn left." The NS app shows me a line on a map. A static line. If I am standing at Steenwijk station, the NS app line doesn't help me because I don't know which way is north. The blue dot in Google Maps moves with me, so I can just follow it.

**Interviewer:** So each app has its strengths?

**P09:** Yes, exactly. The NS app is better for the train. The platform number, the real-time status, the delay information, the ticket purchase. Google Maps is better for walking and for knowing where you are in a city. If someone could take the train information from NS and the walking navigation from Google Maps and put them together... that would be the perfect app. That is what a tourist needs.

But I want to say something more. The biggest problem is not which app is better. The biggest problem is that I didn't know the NS app could do this. I have been using the app for four days. Four days! And the journey planner was there the whole time, and I didn't know. Because the word was in Dutch, because there was no introduction or tutorial, because the app didn't say "hey, are you a tourist? Let me show you how to plan your trip." Nothing. I had to come to this interview for someone to show me. That is the real problem.

**Interviewer:** If you could change one thing about the NS app for tourists, what would it be?

**P09:** Make it visible. The journey planner feature, make it impossible to miss. When a tourist opens the app for the first time, there should be a welcome screen. "Welcome to the Netherlands! Plan your journey here." Big button, clear English, maybe even a little tutorial: "step 1, type your destination. Step 2, choose your train. Step 3, buy your ticket." Three steps. Simple. Because we don't know. We are tourists. We don't know what the app can do. We don't know the Dutch words. We need help.

And the language, it should be automatic. My phone is set to English. The app should see this and switch to English. I should not have to search in settings. Settings is where you go to change your password, not to change the language of an app. The language should be automatic.

**Interviewer:** Any other improvements you'd suggest?

**P09:** Yes, the OV-chipkaart situation. Nobody explain this to me. I still don't understand everything. Can I use it on the bus? On the tram? In every city or only in Amsterdam? How much deposit do I need? What happens if I forget to check out? The NS app should explain this. Not in a FAQ that nobody reads, but in the app itself, when you plan a route. If the route includes a bus, the app should say: "you can use your OV-chipkaart on this bus, make sure to check in when you board." This kind of practical information, for a tourist, is gold. Pure gold.

And one more thing. The announcements in the train, when there is a delay or a change of platform, they are almost always in Dutch first and then maybe in English. But the English version is shorter and sometimes I feel like they leave out important information. It would be amazing if the NS app could give me those announcements as push notifications, in English, in real time. "Your train to Den Haag is delayed by 5 minutes. You will now arrive at 14:35." That would be so much better than trying to understand a Dutch announcement on a crackling speaker.

**Interviewer:** Those are excellent suggestions. One last question: would you recommend the NS app to another tourist?

**P09:** If they know about the journey planner, and if the app is in English, yes. Absolutely, one hundred percent. Because the train information is really good. The platform number, the real-time delays, buying the ticket in the app -- that is better than Google Maps for trains. Much better. But I would have to explain to them: go to settings, change the language to English, and then use the Reisplanner button. Because they will not discover this by themselves. I didn't. And I am a young person who uses apps every day. If I can't discover it, imagine a tourist who is fifty or sixty years old and not so comfortable with technology. They have no chance.

So my recommendation to NS: put a big sign at Schiphol airport, and at Amsterdam Centraal, and at every major tourist point, that says "Download the NS app. Plan your trip in English. Buy your tickets. Know your platform." Simple message. Because we tourists, we need help on the first day. After that, we figure it out. But the first day, it is very, very hard. And right now, NS is invisible on the first day. Google Maps is what everyone uses. And that is a missed opportunity, because the NS app is actually better for trains. They just need to tell people about it.

**Interviewer:** Thank you so much for your time and your openness. This has been incredibly valuable.

**P09:** Thank you! I enjoyed it. And now I will use the journey planner for my trip to Den Haag tomorrow. My first time using it for real. If it works well, I will tell everyone in my hostel. Tourists talk to each other, you know. One good recommendation and the whole hostel downloads the app. That is the best marketing.
