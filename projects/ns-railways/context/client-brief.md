# Client Brief -- NS Multimodal Journey Planner Evaluation

## From: Product Team, NS App
## Date: 2026-03-15

---

## Background

NS launched its multimodal journey planner as a major update to the NS app in late 2025. The planner combines train, bus, tram, metro, and walking directions into a single integrated view -- a significant departure from the traditional NS app experience, which focused exclusively on train travel. The goal was to position NS as the go-to mobility app for the Netherlands, covering the full door-to-door journey rather than just the rail segment.

The feature was developed over 18 months in collaboration with other Dutch public transport operators and uses real-time departure data from multiple sources. It integrates OV-chipkaart and OV-pay information, shows transfers between modes with walking times, and provides disruption alerts across all included transport types.

## Current Situation

Early adoption metrics present a mixed picture. Among passengers who discover the planner, engagement is strong -- they return to it regularly and report high satisfaction in in-app surveys. However, overall feature adoption remains lower than projected. Approximately 15% of active NS app users have tried the multimodal planner since launch. Certain segments -- notably daily commuters on fixed routes -- show almost no engagement with the feature.

Meanwhile, NS faces competitive pressure. Google Maps already offers multimodal directions in the Netherlands, and 9292 (the established Dutch public transport journey planner) remains the default choice for many passengers planning trips that involve non-train modes. NS needs to understand whether low adoption reflects a discoverability problem, a relevance problem, or something else entirely.

## The Dutch Public Transport Landscape

The Netherlands has a dense, integrated public transport network, but it is operated by multiple companies: NS for mainline rail, regional operators (Arriva, Connexxion, Keolis) for bus and regional rail, and municipal operators (GVB in Amsterdam, RET in Rotterdam, HTM in The Hague) for metro and tram. The OV-chipkaart serves as a unified payment system, but journey planning has historically been fragmented. 9292 is the national multimodal planner, while individual operators maintain their own apps. NS entering the multimodal space is a strategic bet that passengers would prefer a single, trusted app over juggling multiple tools.

## What We Are Asking KOOS To Do

We want KOOS to conduct evaluative research with a representative mix of train passengers to understand how the multimodal journey planner is experienced in practice. We want to know: who is finding it and who is not? What do passengers think when they use it? How does it compare to alternatives they already rely on? Where does it fall short? And critically -- does it deliver on the promise of making the door-to-door journey easier? We need honest, evidence-based answers to guide the next iteration of the product before a broader marketing push.
