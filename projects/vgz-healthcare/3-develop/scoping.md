## Research Questions

1. Does the portal concept meet real patient needs as identified in the Discover and Define phases?
2. Which features generate the most perceived value for policyholders, and which feel redundant or unwanted?
3. Who does this concept work for and who does it fail -- across age groups, health situations, and digital confidence levels?
4. What is missing from the current concept that patients expect or need?
5. Would patients trust this portal with their health data, given VGZ's role as an insurer rather than a care provider?

---

## Hypotheses

1. Patients want more self-service control over their health insurance administration and will prefer a portal over phone-based support for routine tasks.
2. A single unified portal will reduce the need for phone contact by making common information (claim status, provider coverage) immediately accessible.
3. A digital-first approach works for the majority of VGZ's policyholder population, including older adults with basic smartphone competence.

## Interview Guide Summary

Participants are shown the clickable Figma prototype in a semi-structured interview setting. The session covers:

- **First impressions** -- Initial reactions to the portal concept before any guided exploration. What do participants notice, expect, and feel?
- **Feature-by-feature walkthrough** -- Guided exploration of each feature (claims tracker, medication management, care provider finder, referral letter upload). Task-based scenarios for each feature.
- **Trust and privacy** -- How participants feel about their health insurer holding and displaying this data. Concerns about data security, sharing with third parties, and the 2024 data breach context.
- **Comparison with current experience** -- How the portal compares to current ways of managing health insurance administration (phone, website, paper).
- **Willingness to adopt** -- Whether participants would use this portal, how often, and what would need to change for them to switch from their current approach.
- **What is missing** -- Unprompted exploration of gaps, unmet needs, and features participants expected but did not find.

---

## Known Constraints or Biases

- **Recruitment bias:** All participants are existing VGZ customers. People who left VGZ or chose a competitor are not represented -- their reasons for leaving may be relevant.
- **Prototype limitations:** The prototype is clickable but not functional. Participants are reacting to a simulated experience, not a working product. Data shown in the prototype is fictional, which may affect perceptions of usefulness and trust.
- **Language:** All interviews are conducted in Dutch. Non-Dutch-speaking policyholders are not represented.
- **Moderator influence:** Two Koos researchers moderate all sessions, introducing consistent framing. Prototype demonstration order is fixed (claims, medication, provider finder, referral), which may create an order effect.
- **Sample size:** 10 participants -- sufficient for qualitative pattern identification, not for quantitative generalisation.

## Additional Context

This concept testing phase builds directly on two completed phases. The Discover phase surfaced unmet patient needs through exploratory research, and the Define phase synthesized these into design principles and opportunity areas that shaped the portal concept. Analysis should evaluate whether the portal successfully addresses the needs identified earlier -- not just whether participants like it.

VGZ stakeholders have differing views on rollout strategy. The Product Owner favours launching all four features simultaneously, while medical advisors and the Head of Customer Experience prefer a phased approach starting with claims tracking. Concept testing findings should provide evidence to inform this decision.

Prior Koos work with VGZ (13% NPS increase, pharmacy toolkit in 400+ pharmacies) gives the team credibility and existing domain knowledge. This is not a cold-start engagement -- there is institutional context and trust to build on.

The medication management feature is the highest-risk element. Medical advisors have flagged concerns about patient self-management, data accuracy, and liability. Pay close attention to how participants respond to this feature, particularly those managing chronic conditions with multiple prescriptions.
