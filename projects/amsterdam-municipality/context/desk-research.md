# Desk Research -- Amsterdam Municipal Services

## Compiled: 2026-03-20
## Sources: Amsterdam Open Data, Gemeente Amsterdam Annual Report 2025, CBS (Statistics Netherlands), internal municipality data shared by Digital Team

---

## Citizen Satisfaction Scores

The municipality conducts an annual citizen satisfaction survey (Burgerpeiling). Most recent results (2025):

| Service Area | Satisfaction (1-10) | Change vs. 2023 |
|---|---|---|
| Overall municipal services | 6.4 | -0.2 |
| Permits (vergunningen) | 5.8 | -0.4 |
| Reporting issues (meldingen) | 6.1 | -0.1 |
| Social services | 5.6 | -0.3 |
| Civil affairs (burgerzaken) | 7.2 | +0.1 |
| Waste management | 6.0 | -0.3 |

Civil affairs (passport renewals, birth certificates) scores consistently higher because the process is relatively simple and well-digitized. All other areas are declining.

Utrecht leads on both satisfaction and digital adoption. Their "Digital met een Menselijk Gezicht" (Digital with a Human Face) program has been cited as a model. Rotterdam's recent service desk redesign improved their score by 0.4 points in one year.

## Digital Channel Usage

MijnAmsterdam portal statistics (2025):

- **Registered users:** 412,000 (approximately 48% of adult population)
- **Monthly active users:** 138,000
- **Most-used services online:** Parking permits (78% digital), waste container requests (71% digital), civil affairs appointments (65% digital)
- **Least-used services online:** Social services applications (23% digital), building permits (31% digital), reporting issues with feedback tracking (34% digital)
- **Drop-off rates:** 42% of users who start a permit application online do not complete it. For social services applications, the drop-off rate is 61%.

## Known Pain Points from Prior Surveys

The municipality's 2024 qualitative survey (n=320) identified recurring themes:

1. **Permit process takes too long.** Citizens report waiting 8-14 weeks for decisions on processes that should take 4-6 weeks. Lack of status updates causes anxiety and repeated calls to the service desk.
2. **Reporting issues gets no feedback.** Citizens report potholes, broken streetlights, or graffiti and never hear what happened. This erodes trust and reduces willingness to report future issues.
3. **Social services application is confusing.** The application process requires documents that citizens do not have or do not know how to obtain. Language is bureaucratic. Citizens report feeling judged rather than helped.
4. **Inconsistent information across channels.** What the website says, what the service desk says, and what a letter says are sometimes different. Citizens do not know which to trust.
5. **Physical service desks are hard to reach.** Reduced opening hours post-COVID have not been fully restored. Citizens in Noord and Nieuw-West have limited access.

## Demographic Context

Amsterdam population profile relevant to service design:

- **Age distribution:** 18% under 18, 32% aged 18-34, 28% aged 35-54, 14% aged 55-74, 8% aged 75+
- **Migration background:** Approximately 56% of residents have a migration background (first or second generation). Largest groups: Surinamese, Moroccan, Turkish, Indonesian, and a growing population from EU member states and the Middle East.
- **Language:** While Dutch is the official language, an estimated 15-20% of adult residents have limited Dutch proficiency. English is widely spoken but not universal -- it is concentrated in the center and south.
- **Digital literacy:** According to CBS estimates adapted to Amsterdam's demographics, approximately 18% of Amsterdam adults (roughly 110,000 people) have low digital skills. This group skews older (55+) and correlates with lower education levels and non-Western migration background, though there are significant exceptions in every demographic group.
- **Internet access:** 94% of Amsterdam households have broadband internet. However, approximately 6% rely solely on mobile internet, which makes complex form-filling difficult.

## Implications for Research

The desk research suggests that the biggest opportunities lie in the three areas the stakeholders selected: permits, reporting, and social services. The data also highlights that digital literacy and language are critical variables -- any research that does not account for these dimensions will miss the most vulnerable citizens. The gap between registration (412,000) and active use (138,000) of MijnAmsterdam suggests that the problem is not awareness but usability and trust.
