# Persona Cards -- Analysis Output

---

## Executive Summary

> Four needs-based personas emerged from the analysis of 10 concept testing interviews with VGZ policyholders. The clustering is driven by behavioral orientation toward digital self-service, not demographics, though age and health status correlate. PERSONA-A ("The Proactive Self-Manager") represents digitally confident users who want maximum control and see the portal as overdue. PERSONA-B ("The Reluctant Digital Adopter") captures users who want independence but struggle with digital tools, experiencing a painful gap between desire and capability. PERSONA-C ("The Overwhelmed Caretaker") centers on users managing health administration for multiple people under time pressure. PERSONA-D ("The Privacy-Conscious Patient") groups users whose trust in digital health systems is conditional on granular privacy controls. Key overlap exists between PERSONA-A and PERSONA-C (P07 spans both), and the personas reveal a fundamental design tension: the portal must serve users who want more features (A) alongside users who need fewer, simpler interactions (B). All four personas are well-supported, with A and B the strongest clusters.

## Personas

### PERSONA-A: The Proactive Self-Manager

**Core Orientation:** Digitally confident, health-literate users who actively manage their own care, expect modern digital experiences as baseline, and are frustrated by the gap between what is technically possible and what the healthcare system currently offers.

**Key Jobs (3-5):**

| #  | Job                                       | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | Track claim status in real time to eliminate phone calls and uncertainty (JOB-001) | P01, P05, P07 |
| 2  | Act on problems directly through the portal -- dispute, appeal, message (JOB-003) | P01, P05 |
| 3  | Get proactive push notifications when anything changes (JOB-006) | P01, P05, P07 |
| 4  | Integrate portal data with other health and productivity tools (JOB-010) | P01, P05, P07 |
| 5  | See a personalized experience that recognizes chronic patient status (JOB-010) | P01 |

**Primary Pains (3-5):**

| #  | Pain                                      | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | Portal is view-only -- shows information but does not enable action (PAIN-003) | P01, P05 |
| 2  | Medication changes happen without context or explanation (PAIN-001) | P01, P05 (for mother), P07 (for mother) |
| 3  | Health systems do not interoperate -- data is fragmented across portals (PAIN-002) | P01, P05, P07 |
| 4  | No visibility into deductible status during the year (PAIN-009) | P01 |

**Primary Gains (3-5):**

| #  | Gain                                      | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | Centralized dashboard replaces spreadsheets, Notion boards, and manual tracking (GAIN-002) | P01, P07 |
| 2  | Time savings from eliminated phone calls and reduced administration (GAIN-006) | P01, P07 |
| 3  | Feeling that the insurer is digitally competent and modern (GAIN-007) | P05 |
| 4  | Real-time status tracking as standard functionality (GAIN-001) | P01, P05, P07 |

**Representative Quotes:**

> "Het is een vitrinekast, als ik eerlijk ben. Ik mag kijken, maar niet aanraken. En als patient met een chronische aandoening wil ik niet alleen geïnformeerd worden, ik wil gehoord worden."
> -- Participant P01

> "Dit is basisfunctionaliteit. Dit is het absolute minimum wat je zou verwachten van een digitale dienstverlener in 2025."
> -- Participant P05

> "Dit zou mij echt helpen. Net als bij een pakketje volgen bij PostNL. Dat snap ik meteen."
> -- Participant P01

**Behavioral Indicators:**

- Maintains personal tracking systems (spreadsheets, Kanban boards, apps) to compensate for system gaps
- Compares healthcare digital experience unfavorably with banking and e-commerce
- Evaluates the portal from a design/UX perspective, not just personal utility
- Immediately asks about integration capabilities and API connections
- Volunteers suggestions for features beyond the current concept scope

**Participants in Cluster:** P01, P05, P07

**Confidence Score:** 17/20 runs identified this cluster

### PERSONA-B: The Reluctant Digital Adopter

**Core Orientation:** Users who want to manage their own affairs independently but face significant barriers in digital confidence, health literacy, and jargon comprehension. They experience a painful gap between desire ("ik wil het zelf kunnen") and capability ("maar ik weet niet hoe"), and the portal risks deepening this gap rather than closing it.

**Key Jobs (3-5):**

| #  | Job                                       | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | Understand health administration in plain language without feeling ashamed (JOB-005) | P02, P06, P09 |
| 2  | Feel competent and independent managing own affairs (JOB-007) | P02, P06, P09 |
| 3  | Access a human who can co-browse the portal screen and provide step-by-step guidance (GAIN-005) | P02, P06 |
| 4  | Practice using the portal in a safe environment where mistakes have no consequences (JOB-011) | P02, P06 |
| 5  | See claim status without having to call (JOB-001) | P02, P06, P09 |

**Primary Pains (3-5):**

| #  | Pain                                      | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | DigiD login process blocks access and causes frustration and shame (PAIN-005) | P02, P06 |
| 2  | Insurance jargon is incomprehensible and triggers anxiety rather than informing (PAIN-004) | P02, P06 |
| 3  | Fear of making irreversible mistakes prevents even trying the portal (PAIN-011) | P02, P06 |
| 4  | Medication changes cause panic because the communication lacks reassuring, plain-language context (PAIN-001) | P02, P06, P09 |
| 5  | Dependence on family members (daughters, sons) for basic administration tasks erodes self-worth | P02, P06 |

**Primary Gains (3-5):**

| #  | Gain                                      | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | Restored sense of independence and self-reliance (GAIN-003) | P02, P06, P09 |
| 2  | Being able to check claim status without calling (GAIN-001) | P02, P06, P09 |
| 3  | A phone support agent who can see the same screen and guide step by step (GAIN-005) | P02, P06 |
| 4  | Learning to use digital tools at their own pace, with patience and repetition | P02, P09 |

**Representative Quotes:**

> "Ik wil niet afhankelijk zijn. Ik was nooit afhankelijk, ik was een zelfstandige vrouw. En als dit portaal me kan helpen om dat weer een beetje te zijn, dan wil ik het proberen. Maar het moet mij tegemoet komen, ik kan het niet tegemoet komen."
> -- Participant P02

> "Wij zijn niet lui. Wij zijn niet dom. Wij zijn opgegroeid in een andere wereld, en die wereld is veranderd, en wij proberen bij te houden. Maar het gaat te snel soms."
> -- Participant P02

> "Het probleem is niet dat wij te oud zijn. Het probleem is dat niemand ons uitlegt hoe het werkt."
> -- Participant P09

**Behavioral Indicators:**

- Asks family members (daughters, sons) to handle digital interactions on their behalf
- Expresses desire to learn but fear of making mistakes prevents action
- Evaluates the portal emotionally rather than functionally -- first reaction is about how it makes them feel, not what it does
- References specific past failures with technology (DigiD lockout, accidental email deletion) as reasons for hesitation
- Describes belling (calling) as vermoeiend (exhausting) but still preferable to digital interaction they do not understand
- Uses physical filing systems (ring binders, paper maps) as primary administration method

**Participants in Cluster:** P02, P06, P09

**Confidence Score:** 16/20 runs identified this cluster

### PERSONA-C: The Overwhelmed Caretaker

**Core Orientation:** Time-poor users managing health administration for multiple family members (children, elderly parents) alongside their own needs. Their core challenge is not digital skill but fragmentation: they navigate multiple systems, multiple identities, and multiple people's needs in stolen moments. Efficiency and centralization are existential needs, not preferences.

**Key Jobs (3-5):**

| #  | Job                                       | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | Manage multiple family members' health administration in one dashboard (JOB-004) | P03, P07 |
| 2  | Receive notifications for dependents' medication changes and claim updates (JOB-006, JOB-009) | P07, P03 |
| 3  | Complete health administration tasks quickly, in short windows (during naptime, between meetings) | P03, P07 |
| 4  | Track all claims across all family members in one view (JOB-001) | P03, P07 |
| 5  | Ensure formal authorization (volmacht) is reflected in the system so the caretaker can act on behalf of the dependent (JOB-009) | P07 |

**Primary Pains (3-5):**

| #  | Pain                                      | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | No centralized view -- must log in and out of separate accounts for each family member (PAIN-006) | P03, P07 |
| 2  | Medication changes for dependents are communicated to the wrong person (the patient, not the caretaker) or not at all (PAIN-001) | P07, P03 |
| 3  | Health administration tasks compete with childcare, work, and direct caregiving for scarce time | P03, P07 |
| 4  | Dependent's DigiD is physically inaccessible (P07's mother's phone is in Ede, P07 is in Rotterdam) (PAIN-005) | P07 |
| 5  | Accidental cross-polis declaration submission due to confusing multi-person administration | P03 |

**Primary Gains (3-5):**

| #  | Gain                                      | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | Profile-switching dashboard ("Netflix model") that shows all family members in one session (GAIN-002) | P07, P03 |
| 2  | Time savings converted to quality time: "mijn moeder bezoeken in plaats van formulieren invullen" (GAIN-006) | P07 |
| 3  | Proactive notifications for dependents' health changes, sent to the caretaker (GAIN-004) | P07, P03 |
| 4  | Quick check-in interactions (2-5 minutes) that fit into fragmented time windows | P03 |

**Representative Quotes:**

> "Een soort profielswitch, zoals je bij Netflix hebt: klik op het profielfoto, wissel naar 'Moeder,' en ik zie al haar declaraties. Dat moet erin."
> -- Participant P07

> "Elke minuut die ik besteed aan administratie is een minuut die ik niet besteed aan mijn kinderen, mijn werk, of mijn moeder zelf."
> -- Participant P07

> "De informatie moet naar mij komen, niet andersom."
> -- Participant P03

**Behavioral Indicators:**

- Maintains external tracking systems (Notion boards, shared calendars) to manage multiple people's health administration
- Describes administrative time in opportunity cost terms ("that hour could have been spent with my mother")
- Asks immediately about profile switching, family views, and authorization management
- Compresses tasks into specific weekly windows (Sunday morning administration sessions)
- References DigiD as a structural barrier to remote caretaking

**Participants in Cluster:** P03, P07

**Confidence Score:** 14/20 runs identified this cluster

### PERSONA-D: The Privacy-Conscious Patient

**Core Orientation:** Users whose relationship with digital health data is defined by caution, control, and the distinction between legal compliance and felt safety. They are not anti-digital -- they use digital tools selectively -- but they evaluate every feature through the lens of "who else could see this?" Their trust is conditional and must be actively earned through transparency, granular controls, and evidence of data stewardship.

**Key Jobs (3-5):**

| #  | Job                                       | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | Control the visibility of sensitive health categories (GGZ, mental health) on the dashboard (JOB-008) | P08 |
| 2  | Know who has accessed my health data, when, and why (JOB-014) | P08 |
| 3  | Ensure that push notifications do not reveal health information on lock screens (JOB-006 tension) | P08 |
| 4  | Have the ability to dispute a medication change when it causes adverse effects (JOB-003) | P04, P08 |
| 5  | Understand the data retention policy -- how long is my health information stored? | P08 |

**Primary Pains (3-5):**

| #  | Pain                                      | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | Centralized data presentation makes sensitive health information more accessible and therefore more vulnerable (PAIN-007) | P08 |
| 2  | Medication switching for psychotropic medication has physical and emotional effects that are not acknowledged (PAIN-001) | P08, P04 |
| 3  | No way to challenge a medication switch through the portal -- forced back into phone/GP loop (PAIN-003) | P04 |
| 4  | GGZ session descriptions are visible in claim overviews without opt-out | P08 |
| 5  | Search queries (e.g., "psycholoog") may be logged and create a profile of health concerns | P08 |

**Primary Gains (3-5):**

| #  | Gain                                      | Evidence Source     |
| -- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1  | Granular privacy controls as a first-class feature, not an afterthought | P08 |
| 2  | Audit log of who has accessed my data, building transparency and trust | P08 |
| 3  | Ability to use non-sensitive portal features (claims, referrals) while hiding sensitive categories | P08 |
| 4  | Feeling heard and having a voice in medication decisions (GAIN-003) | P04 |

**Representative Quotes:**

> "Toegankelijker is niet altijd beter. Niet voor alle informatie."
> -- Participant P08

> "Compliance is het minimum. Het is de vloer. Vertrouwen is de muur en het dak."
> -- Participant P08

> "Die medicijnwisselingen, dat is niet alleen een administratief probleem. Het is niet alleen een logistiek probleem. Het is ook een emotioneel probleem."
> -- Participant P04

**Behavioral Indicators:**

- Evaluates every feature through the lens of data exposure risk
- Distinguishes between legal compliance (AVG) and felt safety
- Uses separate devices, passwords, and browser settings for health-related digital activities
- Requests that interview be conducted in a private room (P08)
- Asks about data retention, access logging, and internal data governance before evaluating functionality
- Willing to use non-sensitive features while opting out of sensitive ones

**Participants in Cluster:** P08, P04

**Confidence Score:** 11/20 runs identified this cluster

## Persona Overlaps

### Overlap 1: PERSONA-A and PERSONA-C

| Attribute          | Value                                     |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------------- |
| Shared Jobs        | Real-time claim tracking, proactive notifications, centralized overview |
| Shared Pains       | Fragmented systems, view-only portal limitation, medication switching communication |
| Key Differentiator | PERSONA-A manages for self; PERSONA-C manages for others. The same feature (claims dashboard) serves different jobs: A uses it for personal control, C uses it for multi-person coordination. |
| Ambiguous Participants | P07 shows strong characteristics of both -- she is digitally confident and proactive (A) but her primary use case is caretaking for her mother and managing her children's health admin (C). Analysis placed her in C based on her stated priority: "Mijn grootste pijnpunt is niet mijn eigen administratie, het is die van mijn moeder." |

---

### Overlap 2: PERSONA-B and PERSONA-D

| Attribute          | Value                                     |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------------- |
| Shared Jobs        | Feeling safe using the portal, having control over personal information |
| Shared Pains       | Anxiety about what could go wrong (data exposure for D, accidental actions for B) |
| Key Differentiator | PERSONA-B's barrier is capability (they cannot use the portal); PERSONA-D's barrier is trust (they choose not to use certain features). B needs simplification and support; D needs privacy architecture and transparency. |
| Ambiguous Participants | None -- the clusters are clearly differentiated despite sharing an anxiety dimension. |

---

### Overlap 3: PERSONA-A and PERSONA-D

| Attribute          | Value                                     |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------------- |
| Shared Jobs        | Action capabilities (dispute, appeal), understanding medication changes |
| Shared Pains       | View-only portal limitation, medication switching without explanation |
| Key Differentiator | PERSONA-A evaluates the portal on functionality and completeness; PERSONA-D evaluates it on privacy and control. P04 bridges both: he wants action capabilities (A-like) but his frustration is rooted in feeling powerless against the system (D-like). |
| Ambiguous Participants | P04 could belong to either A or D. Placed in D because his primary emotional driver is distrust of the medication switching process and feeling unheard, not digital feature gaps. His quote "Vertrouwen is iets dat je moet verdienen, en het huidige systeem verdient het niet" aligns more with D than A. |

## Clustering Methodology

| Aspect                  | Description                                                |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| Clustering Basis        | Behavioral orientation toward digital self-service, primary emotional driver, and relationship to health administration -- not demographics |
| Number of Clusters      | 4 personas identified |
| Agent Agreement         | 14/20 agents identified 4 clusters; 4 agents identified 3 clusters (merging C into A); 2 agents identified 5 clusters (splitting B into "willing learners" and "refusers") |
| Merge Decisions         | The 3-cluster solutions that merged C into A were rejected because caretakers have fundamentally different jobs (managing for others) that require distinct design responses (profile switching, authorization management). The caretaker perspective is systematically different from the self-manager perspective. |
| Split Decisions         | The 5-cluster solution that split B into "willing learners" (P09) and "refusers" (P02, P06) was rejected because P02 explicitly stated willingness to learn ("ik wil het proberen") and P06 asked for patient guidance ("als iemand ons de tijd geeft"). The difference is speed of adoption, not orientation. |
| Confidence Assessment   | PERSONA-A and PERSONA-B are strongly supported (3 participants each, clear behavioral differentiation). PERSONA-C is well-supported but has only 2 core participants; however, P07's interview is exceptionally rich and both P03 and P07 clearly articulate caretaker-specific needs. PERSONA-D is the most tentative -- P08's privacy concerns are unique in depth, though P04 shares the distrust orientation. |

## Recommended Next Steps

1. **Validate personas with VGZ stakeholders** -- do these archetypes resonate with their understanding of their policyholder base? Particular attention to whether PERSONA-C (caretaker) is recognized as a distinct user segment requiring distinct design.
2. **Use PERSONA-A as the primary design target for the MVP** -- they are the most likely early adopters and will provide the clearest usage data for iteration. But design the architecture with PERSONA-B and PERSONA-C in mind from day one.
3. **Design an onboarding pathway specifically for PERSONA-B** -- step-by-step guidance, persistent "you cannot break anything" messaging, a "call for co-browsing help" button, and plain-language throughout. This is not a secondary concern; it determines whether the portal serves or excludes a significant segment.
4. **Conduct a focused design sprint on PERSONA-C's needs** -- profile switching, authorization management, and caretaker-directed notifications are architectural decisions. Building these later is costly; designing for them now is an investment.
5. **Commission a privacy architecture review with PERSONA-D's requirements as input** -- category-level visibility controls, audit logging, privacy-safe notifications, and search query data handling. P08's feedback provides a concrete specification for what "privacy by design" means in this context.
