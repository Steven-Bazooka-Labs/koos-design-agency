# Jobs to Be Done -- Analysis Output

---

## Executive Summary

> This analysis of 10 concept testing interviews with VGZ policyholders reveals 14 distinct jobs across three confidence tiers. The dominant pattern is a universal need for real-time visibility into claim status (19/20 runs), closely followed by the need to understand and control medication changes (18/20). A critical tension emerged between the desire for comprehensive digital self-service and the inability of the current concept to support action -- participants can see information but cannot dispute, appeal, or communicate through the portal. The emotional and social lenses uncovered jobs invisible to surface-level analysis: the desire to feel competent managing one's own health administration (particularly among older participants and young parents), and the need for privacy controls granular enough to protect sensitive health categories. The adversarial lens flagged that the portal risks deepening the digital divide rather than closing it, as those who most need self-service tools are least equipped to use them. Six high-confidence jobs, four medium-confidence, and four low-confidence findings are detailed below.

## High Confidence Findings (12+ of 20 runs)

### JOB-001: When I submit a health insurance claim, I want to track its status in real time, so I can stop wondering whether it was received and when I will be reimbursed.

| Attribute         | Value                                      |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Confidence Score  | 19/20 runs                                |
| Lens Breakdown    | Obvious 5/5, Implicit 5/5, Emotional 5/5, Adversarial 4/5 |
| Type              | Functional                                 |

**Supporting Evidence:**

> "Nu moet ik bellen om te vragen waar mijn declaratie is. Of ik zoek in Mijn VGZ maar daar staat alleen 'in behandeling' of 'afgehandeld.' Geen tussenliggende stappen, geen verwachte doorlooptijd, niets."
> -- Participant P01, identified by agents O1, O2, O3, O4, O5, I1, I2, I3, I4, I5, E1, E2, E3, E5, A1, A2, A3, A4

> "Ik dien ze in, digitaal via Mijn VGZ of soms per post als het digitaal niet lukt, en dan verdwijnen ze in een zwart gat."
> -- Participant P10, identified by agents O1, O2, O3, O4, O5, I1, I2, I4, I5, E1, E3, E4, E5, A1, A2, A3

> "Want nu is het een zwart gat. Ik dien iets in en dan hoor ik weken niets. En dan ga ik me zorgen maken: heb ik het goed gedaan?"
> -- Participant P03, identified by agents O1, O2, O3, O4, I1, I2, I3, I5, E1, E2, E3, E4, A1, A2

**Notes:** This was the strongest finding in the entire analysis. All 10 participants expressed this need, many using the metaphor of package tracking (PostNL). The adversarial lens noted that status visibility without action options creates a "beautiful frustration" -- you can see the problem but not fix it. One adversarial agent (A5) challenged whether status visibility alone reduces call volume, arguing it may increase anxiety in some users.

| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Confidence Score  | 11/20 runs                                |

**Supporting Evidence:**

> "Ik voel me soms gewoon niet competent genoeg om het allemaal te regelen."

> -- Participant P03, identified by agents E1, E2, E3, E4, E5, I3, A1

### JOB-008: When my sensitive health information (mental health, stigmatized conditions) is in a digital system, I want granular control over what is visible and to whom, so I can protect my privacy without losing access to the service.

| Attribute         | Value                                      |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Confidence Score  | 10/20 runs                                |
| Lens Breakdown    | Obvious 2/5, Implicit 2/5, Emotional 3/5, Adversarial 3/5 |
| Type              | Emotional                                  |

**Supporting Evidence:**

> "Ik wil dat ik kan kiezen welke declaraties hier zichtbaar zijn. Op het hoofdscherm, bedoel ik."
> -- Participant P08, identified by agents O2, O4, I2, I5, E1, E2, E4, A1, A3, A4

> "Laat mij bepalen wat zichtbaar is en wat niet. Geef mij de controle."
> -- Participant P08, identified by agents O4, I2, I5, E1, E2, E3, A1, A3

> "Als iemand mijn gegevens steelt, dan weten ze alles over mij. Mijn hartfalen. Mijn medicijnen. Mijn adres. En ik ben een vrouw alleen."
> -- Participant P06, identified by agents I3, I4, E1, E3, E4, E5, A2, A4

**Human Review Note:** This finding was primarily driven by P08 (mental health) and P06 (fear of data exposure). The adversarial lens challenged whether this represents a widespread need or an edge case. The consensus was that while P08's specific request (GGZ category hiding) is a niche use case, the underlying desire for data control is broadly relevant and likely to grow as more people access mental health services.

**Notes:** P08 requested privacy settings as a "first-class feature" visible at first login, not buried in settings. This represents a design philosophy challenge: most portals default to showing everything, whereas P08 argues the default should be concealment for sensitive categories.

## Tensions & Conflicts

### Tension 1: Self-Service Control vs. Digital Exclusion

| Jobs in Tension   | JOB-001/JOB-003 vs. JOB-005/JOB-007       |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Nature            | The portal promises self-service control and transparency, but the people who need it most (P02, P06) cannot use it due to digital literacy barriers and jargon. More digital self-service may deepen the divide rather than close it. |
| Affected Segments | PERSONA-A (benefits) vs. PERSONA-B (excluded) |
| Implication       | The portal must be designed for the lowest common denominator of digital skill, or accompanied by human-assisted channels that bridge the gap. A parallel phone service that can co-browse the portal (requested by P01, P02, P06) could address this. |

---

### Tension 2: Transparency vs. Privacy

| Jobs in Tension   | JOB-001/JOB-002 vs. JOB-008/JOB-014       |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Nature            | Centralizing health information in one visible dashboard serves transparency (what P01, P04 want) but threatens privacy (what P08 fears). The same feature -- showing all claims in one view -- is valued by one segment and feared by another. |
| Affected Segments | P01, P04, P05, P07 (want full visibility) vs. P08 (wants selective visibility) |
| Implication       | Default visibility must be configurable per category. GGZ and other sensitive categories should be opt-in for dashboard display, not opt-out. This requires a privacy-first architecture decision. |

---

### Tension 3: Information vs. Action

| Jobs in Tension   | JOB-001/JOB-002 (information) vs. JOB-003 (action) |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Nature            | The concept satisfies the information need but not the action need. Multiple participants (P01, P04, P10) described the portal as a "half solution" that creates awareness of problems without providing resolution tools. P04: "Halve oplossingen zijn erger dan geen oplossingen." |
| Affected Segments | All participants, but most acutely P04 (medication disputes) and P10 (claim disputes) |
| Implication       | If the portal launches as information-only, it must clearly communicate that action capabilities are planned. Otherwise, it risks creating frustration: users see what is wrong but cannot fix it. |

---

### Tension 4: Push Notifications vs. Privacy-Safe Communication

| Jobs in Tension   | JOB-006 vs. JOB-008                       |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Nature            | Proactive notifications serve the majority (JOB-006) but endanger the privacy-sensitive minority (JOB-008). A medication change notification on a lock screen can expose sensitive health information. |
| Affected Segments | Most participants (want push) vs. P08 (needs privacy-safe notifications) |
| Implication       | Notifications must be content-agnostic on lock screens. Generic message ("U heeft een bericht in uw gezondheidsportaal") rather than specific ("Uw antidepressivum is gewijzigd"). This is a technical design requirement, not a preference. |

## Methodology Note

This analysis was produced using Koos's multi-agent analysis protocol. Each transcript was analyzed independently by 20 agents across 4 interpretive lenses:

- **Obvious lens (5 agents):** Surface-level, explicitly stated jobs
- **Implicit lens (5 agents):** Jobs inferred from behavior, workarounds, and context
- **Emotional lens (5 agents):** Jobs rooted in feelings, identity, and social dynamics
- **Adversarial lens (5 agents):** Challenges, stress-tests, and alternative interpretations of proposed jobs

Findings were then consolidated through convergence analysis. Confidence tiers reflect how many of the 20 independent runs surfaced each finding. All quotes were verified against source transcripts and all inferences were audited for evidentiary support.

Total agent runs for this analysis: 43 (20 lens runs + convergence + disagreement deep-dive + 15 verification runs).
