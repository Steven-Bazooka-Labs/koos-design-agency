# Anonymization Guidelines

These guidelines define how participant identities must be protected in all research outputs — notes, transcripts, reports, presentations, and any AI-assisted analysis or synthesis.

---

## Participant Identifiers

Assign each participant a sequential code: P01, P02, P03, and so on. Use these codes consistently across all documents and analyses related to the same study.

- Never use real names, initials, or nicknames in any research output.
- Never use identifiers that encode personal attributes (e.g., "P-Female-35" or "P-Amsterdam-Doctor"). Demographic information should be stored separately from transcript data and linked only by the participant code.
- Maintain a single master lookup table linking participant codes to real identities. Store this separately from research data with restricted access. This table is the only place real names should appear.

---

## Direct Identifiers

Remove all direct identifiers from research data before analysis or reporting. Direct identifiers include:

- **Participant's name** in any form (full name, first name, surname, maiden name)
- **Family members' names** — spouses, children, parents, siblings
- **Contact information** — phone numbers, email addresses, physical addresses
- **Government identifiers** — BSN (Dutch social security), passport numbers, insurance numbers
- **Digital identifiers** — usernames, social media handles, IP addresses
- **Photographs or voice recordings** where the participant is identifiable (unless explicit separate consent exists)

Replace removed identifiers with the marker `[anonymized]` so that readers can see something was redacted without knowing what.

---

## Indirect Identifiers

Indirect identifiers are details that do not name a person directly but could identify them in combination, especially in small samples. Assess and handle the following:

- **Employers and job titles:** Replace specific employers with sector descriptions. "Head of Digital at ING" becomes "senior digital role at a major Dutch bank." Replace unusual job titles with generic equivalents.
- **Neighborhoods and addresses:** Replace with area types. "Jordaan" becomes "an inner-city Amsterdam neighborhood." Remove street names entirely.
- **Schools and universities:** Replace with descriptions. "Montessori Lyceum Amsterdam" becomes "a secondary school in Amsterdam."
- **Hospitals and clinics:** Replace with descriptions. "Amsterdam UMC, location VUmc" becomes "an academic hospital in Amsterdam."
- **Distinctive personal circumstances:** Events, conditions, or situations that are rare enough to be identifying. A participant who mentions being "the only wheelchair user in my department" in a study of 6 employees at one company is potentially identifiable from that detail alone.

---

## Medical Information

When research touches on health topics:

- Generalize specific diagnoses to broader categories. "Stage 2 non-Hodgkin lymphoma" becomes "a serious medical condition" or "a form of cancer," depending on the level of specificity the analysis requires.
- Remove medication names unless they are central to the research question and the sample is large enough to prevent identification.
- Remove names of treating physicians and specific treatment facilities.

---

## Financial Details

- Remove specific financial figures (salaries, debts, savings amounts, mortgage values).
- Replace with ranges or general descriptors when the financial context matters for the analysis. "I earn 85,000 per year" becomes "the participant described a household income above the Dutch median."
- Remove account numbers, policy numbers, and any financial identifiers.

---

## Quote Handling

Quotes included in reports or presentations require additional scrutiny:

- Re-read each quote and ask: could someone who knows this participant recognize them from this quote, even with the name removed?
- Apply `[anonymized]` markers for any identifying details within the quote itself. Example: "When I moved from [anonymized city] to Amsterdam, my doctor at [anonymized] told me..." 
- If a quote contains a distinctive speech pattern, idiom, or anecdote that is strongly associated with a specific individual (and the audience might know that individual), consider paraphrasing instead of direct quotation. Note that the quote has been paraphrased.

---

## Small Sample Risks

In studies with fewer than 10 participants, standard anonymization may be insufficient. Additional precautions:

- Assess whether the combination of demographic details (age, role, location, family situation) included in a report could narrow identification to one or two people, even without names.
- Consider suppressing or generalizing demographic details beyond what would be necessary in a larger sample.
- When presenting findings to the commissioning organization, be aware that internal stakeholders may be able to identify participants from contextual details. This is especially relevant in employee research or research with a small client base.

---

## Cross-Quote Identification

When the same participant is quoted multiple times in a report, the accumulation of details across quotes can enable identification even when each individual quote is safe.

- Review all quotes from a single participant together, not just individually.
- Assess whether the combined details across quotes create an identifiable profile.
- If they do, reduce the identifying details in some quotes or distribute fewer quotes from that participant across the report.
