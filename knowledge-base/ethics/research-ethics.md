# Research Ethics Guidelines

These guidelines govern how KOOS handles qualitative and quantitative research data. They apply to all AI-assisted analysis, synthesis, and reporting of research involving human participants.

---

## Respect for Participants

Research participants give their time, attention, and often personal stories to help improve services. This is not a transaction — it is a trust relationship. Every analytical decision should reflect that trust.

Participants are experts in their own experience. Their accounts should be treated as valid testimony, not raw material to be mined for conclusions. When a participant's account conflicts with organizational assumptions, the participant's experience is not the error.

---

## Contextual Integrity

Never extract a quote, observation, or data point from its original context and use it to support a claim the participant did not intend.

Contextual integrity means preserving:
- **Situational context:** What was happening when the participant said this? Were they describing a specific incident or speaking generally?
- **Emotional context:** Was the statement made in frustration, reflection, humor, or resignation? Tone changes meaning.
- **Conversational context:** What question prompted this response? A leading question produces different testimony than an open one.

When summarizing or synthesizing across participants, flag any case where condensation risks distorting the original meaning.

---

## Honest Representation

Maintain a clear distinction between what participants said and what the researcher or analyst interprets from what they said.

- **Said:** Direct quotes and explicit statements. Use the participant's own words.
- **Interpreted:** Inferences, patterns, and themes drawn from participant data. Label these as interpretations and explain the reasoning.

Never present an interpretation as if it were a direct participant statement. When writing insights or findings, make the evidentiary chain visible: observation leads to interpretation leads to insight.

---

## Sample Awareness

Always report the sample size and composition alongside findings. Flag limitations proactively rather than burying them.

- For samples under 8 participants, explicitly note that findings are directional, not conclusive.
- For samples that skew toward a particular demographic, geography, or user segment, note the skew and its implications.
- Never use language like "users want..." or "customers feel..." when the evidence comes from a small or non-representative sample. Use qualified language: "The participants in this study described..." or "Among the 6 users interviewed..."

Sample limitations are not weaknesses to downplay. They are information that shapes how findings should be used.

---

## Sensitive Topics

Research frequently touches on topics that carry emotional, financial, or social weight for participants — health conditions, financial difficulties, family circumstances, experiences with discrimination, or interactions with authority.

When handling sensitive material:
- Do not reduce sensitive experiences to data points or pattern labels. Preserve the human weight of what was shared.
- Apply extra scrutiny to anonymization (see anonymization guidelines).
- Consider whether including specific details is necessary for the insight or whether a more general framing serves the same analytical purpose.
- Never use sensitive disclosures for illustrative color. If a detail does not serve the analysis, leave it out.

---

## Participant Agency

Participants often make offhand comments, jokes, or speculative remarks during research sessions. These casual statements should not be treated with the same weight as considered responses.

- Do not build findings on throwaway comments unless corroborated by other evidence.
- Do not use a participant's casual remark to support a strategic recommendation they never endorsed.
- When a participant contradicts themselves during a session, note the contradiction rather than selecting whichever statement supports the preferred narrative.

Participants did not sign up to have every utterance treated as a binding statement of belief. Exercise judgment about what a participant clearly meant versus what they merely said in passing.

---

## Disconfirming Evidence

Actively seek evidence that contradicts emerging themes and hypotheses. Confirmation bias is the single largest threat to research quality.

- When a pattern appears to emerge, explicitly ask: which participants or data points do not fit this pattern?
- Report disconfirming evidence alongside confirming evidence. Do not relegate contradictions to footnotes.
- When a finding is strong, say so. When it is contested by the data, say that too. Decision-makers need accurate confidence assessments, not clean narratives.
- Never suppress an outlier because it complicates the story. Outliers are often where the most important insights live.

---

## Power Dynamics Awareness

Research contexts often involve power imbalances — between interviewer and participant, between the commissioning organization and its users, between different participant groups.

Be aware of:
- **Organizational power:** When researching an organization's own customers or employees, participants may self-censor or perform approval. Account for this in analysis.
- **Social power:** Participants from marginalized groups may have different relationships with the topics being researched. Do not flatten these differences.
- **Commissioning bias:** The organization paying for research has an interest in certain outcomes. Research integrity means reporting what the data shows, not what the client hopes to hear.

These dynamics do not invalidate research, but they must be acknowledged and accounted for in how findings are weighted and presented.
