# KOOS Tone of Voice Guide

This guide defines how we write at KOOS — across research deliverables, client communications, internal documentation, and AI-generated outputs. Every piece of writing that carries the KOOS name should reflect these principles.

## Core Principles

### Professional but warm

We write with authority and care. Our tone respects the reader's time and intelligence without resorting to corporate jargon or empty formality. We are approachable experts: the kind of colleagues who explain complex findings clearly over coffee, not the kind who hide behind acronyms and passive constructions.

### Evidence-driven

Every claim we make is grounded in what we observed, heard, or measured. When we interpret beyond the direct evidence, we say so explicitly. We never present speculation as fact. If we are offering a hypothesis, we label it as one. If the evidence is thin, we flag it. Our credibility depends on this discipline.

### Clear and visual

We favour structured layouts over dense paragraphs. Tables, numbered lists, and clear hierarchies help readers find what they need. A well-placed comparison table communicates more than three paragraphs of description. When prose is needed, we keep it concise and scannable.

### Empathetic toward research participants

The people who take part in our research have shared their time, experiences, and often their frustrations. They are not "data points," "subjects," or abstract "users." They are individuals whose stories deserve accurate, respectful representation. We quote them faithfully, identify them by participant ID, and never reduce their experiences to convenient generalisations.

### Dutch directness

We say what we mean. We do not hedge with unnecessary qualifiers, pad with filler sentences, or bury the point under layers of preamble. If a product experience is broken, we say it is broken — with evidence. Directness is not the same as coldness; we are candid because we respect the reader enough to be honest.

### Confident but honest

We state clearly what the evidence shows. We also state clearly what it does not show. Confidence means owning our findings; honesty means owning their limits. Both are non-negotiable.

## Before and After Examples

These examples illustrate the difference between generic writing and KOOS-quality writing.

### 1. Writing about participants

**Don't:**
"Users expressed frustration with the onboarding flow."

**Do:**
"Seven out of ten participants described frustration during onboarding — particularly at the identity verification step, where three abandoned the process entirely."

Why it matters: The revised version specifies how many people experienced the issue, identifies the exact step where friction occurs, and quantifies the severity. This gives the client something actionable, not just a vague sentiment.

### 2. Presenting uncertain findings

**Don't:**
"The data suggests that younger users prefer mobile interfaces."

**Do:**
"Three of the four participants under 30 preferred the mobile app over the website. However, with only four participants in this age group, this pattern needs validation with a larger sample."

Why it matters: The revised version presents the same observation but is transparent about the small sample. We never let a reader walk away with false confidence in a finding.

### 3. Describing pain points

**Don't:**
"There are issues with the communication strategy."

**Do:**
"Participants consistently reported receiving conflicting information from different channels — P03 was told one thing by phone and another by letter, P07 received outdated information via email that contradicted the website."

Why it matters: Concrete examples with participant IDs make the problem real and verifiable. A stakeholder reading this can trace back to the original transcript. Abstract statements like "there are issues" give no one anything to work with.

### 4. Making recommendations

**Don't:**
"The company should consider improving the digital experience to drive customer satisfaction."

**Do:**
"The three highest-impact opportunities based on evidence strength and frequency: (1) Consolidate medication switch communication into a single, clear notification — cited by 8/10 participants. (2) Add real-time status tracking for claims — the most common workaround was calling the helpdesk to ask 'where is my claim?' (3) Simplify the referral letter process — currently requires 3 steps that could be reduced to 1."

Why it matters: Recommendations must be specific, prioritised, and traceable to evidence. "Improve the digital experience" is a direction, not a recommendation. The revised version tells the client exactly what to do, why, and how significant the impact is likely to be.

## Language Preferences

These conventions apply to all KOOS deliverables, whether written by people or generated with AI assistance.

| Prefer | Avoid | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| "Participants" | "Users" or "respondents" | Acknowledges people as active contributors to the research (use "respondents" only for survey-based studies) |
| "Evidence suggests" | "Data shows" | Reflects the interpretive nature of qualitative research |
| "We observed" | "It was observed" | Active voice is clearer and takes ownership of the analysis |
| Participant IDs: "P03 described..." | "One participant described..." | Enables traceability back to source transcripts |
| "Based on [N] participants..." | "Based on our research..." | Specificity builds trust and signals rigour |
| "We recommend" | "It is recommended" | Own the recommendation; do not hide behind passive voice |

### A note on Dutch-language deliverables

When writing in Dutch, apply the same principles. Use active voice ("Wij zagen" not "Er werd waargenomen"), refer to participants by ID, and maintain the same commitment to evidence and directness. The tone should feel equally natural in Dutch as in English — professional, warm, and precise.

---

## Applying This Guide

This tone of voice is not a set of rigid rules; it is a standard to meet. Every sentence we write should pass a simple test: does it tell the reader something specific, supported, and useful? If the answer is no, rewrite it until it does.
