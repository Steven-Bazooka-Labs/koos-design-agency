# Koos Terminology Glossary

This glossary defines key terms as Koos uses them. Consistent terminology prevents miscommunication between researchers, designers, clients, and AI systems. Each entry includes the definition, a correct usage example, and a common misuse to avoid.

---

## Insight

**Definition:** A non-obvious, actionable understanding of human behavior or motivation that changes how you approach a design problem. An insight reveals *why* people behave the way they do, not merely *what* they do.

**Correct usage:** "Our insight is that patients delay contacting their insurer not because the process is complex, but because they fear being denied coverage — so the real barrier is emotional, not procedural."

**Common misuse:** Using "insight" as a synonym for any finding or observation. "Our insight is that 60% of users drop off at step 3" is not an insight — it is a metric. An insight would explain *why* they drop off.

---

## Observation

**Definition:** A factual, descriptive account of something witnessed during research — what a participant did, said, or what was present in their environment. Observations are raw data, free from interpretation.

**Correct usage:** "P04 checked their phone three times while attempting to complete the form, each time returning to re-read the same field label."

**Common misuse:** Embedding interpretation into observations. "P04 was confused by the form" is an interpretation, not an observation. The observation is the behavior; confusion is the analyst's inference.

---

## Finding

**Definition:** A factual statement supported by research data, typically aggregating across multiple observations or data points. Findings describe patterns but do not explain underlying motivations.

**Correct usage:** "Five of eight participants were unable to complete the permit application without external help."

**Common misuse:** Conflating findings with insights. A finding states what the data shows; an insight explains why it matters and what to do about it.

---

## Need

**Definition:** A requirement that a person has in order to achieve a goal or resolve a problem. Needs are expressed from the participant's perspective and are grounded in evidence, not assumed.

**Correct usage:** "Caregivers need a reliable way to track medication schedules across multiple family members, because errors create serious anxiety."

**Common misuse:** Describing a solution as a need. "Users need a dashboard" is a solution, not a need. The need might be: "Users need to see all relevant status information at a glance without navigating between screens."

---

## Pain Point

**Definition:** A specific moment or aspect of an experience where a person encounters frustration, difficulty, confusion, or emotional distress. Pain points are located in the experience, not in the person.

**Correct usage:** "A key pain point is the 48-hour silence after submitting a claim — participants described feeling anxious and abandoned during this window."

**Common misuse:** Using "pain point" so broadly that it loses specificity. "The whole onboarding is a pain point" is too vague. Identify the specific moments that cause friction.

---

## Gain

**Definition:** A specific moment or aspect of an experience where a person derives unexpected value, satisfaction, or delight. Gains are the positive counterpart to pain points.

**Correct usage:** "Participants described a clear gain when the app confirmed their appointment with a human-readable summary — several said it was the first time a healthcare app made them feel confident about next steps."

**Common misuse:** Treating any positive feedback as a gain. A gain should identify a specific moment of value creation, not a general sentiment like "users liked the app."

---

## Job to Be Done (JTBD)

**Definition:** The underlying task or objective a person is trying to accomplish in a given context, expressed independently of any particular product or solution. JTBD focuses on the progress a person wants to make.

**Correct usage:** "When a commuter checks the NS app during a delay, their job to be done is not 'check train times' — it is 'decide whether to wait, reroute, or make alternative plans so I am not late for my commitment.'"

**Common misuse:** Framing JTBD as a feature request or product interaction. "The user's job is to filter search results" describes a UI action, not the progress the person is trying to make in their life.

---

## Moment of Truth

**Definition:** A critical interaction point where a person forms or changes their judgment about a service or organization. Moments of truth are high-stakes — they disproportionately shape overall perception.

**Correct usage:** "The moment of truth in the insurance claim journey is the first response after filing: a personal, clear reply builds trust, while a generic acknowledgment erodes it."

**Common misuse:** Labeling every touchpoint a moment of truth. If everything is critical, nothing is. Reserve this term for interactions where perception genuinely shifts.

---

## Touchpoint

**Definition:** Any point of interaction between a person and a service, organization, or product. Touchpoints can be digital (app, website, email), physical (office visit, printed letter), or human (phone call, in-person conversation).

**Correct usage:** "The journey includes 14 touchpoints across three channels: app, email, and in-branch interactions."

**Common misuse:** Confusing touchpoints with moments of truth. A touchpoint is any interaction; a moment of truth is a touchpoint that carries disproportionate weight. Most touchpoints are routine.

---

## Persona

**Definition:** A research-based archetype representing a distinct segment of users or stakeholders. Personas synthesize patterns across multiple participants into a composite character that guides design decisions. They are tools, not stereotypes.

**Correct usage:** "Our 'Cautious First-Timer' persona represents the segment of users who have never applied for a permit before and approach the process with significant uncertainty about requirements and timelines."

**Common misuse:** Creating personas from assumptions rather than research data, or treating personas as real individuals. Personas are models — they simplify reality to make it actionable. They should never be used to dismiss individual participant experiences that don't fit the archetype.

---

## Journey Map

**Definition:** A visual representation of the steps a person takes to accomplish a goal over time, including their actions, thoughts, emotions, and interactions with a service at each stage. Journey maps are anchored in research data.

**Correct usage:** "The journey map reveals that the emotional low point occurs not during the medical procedure itself, but three days later when the patient is home and uncertain whether their recovery is progressing normally."

**Common misuse:** Creating journey maps from internal assumptions without participant data. A journey map built from how the organization *thinks* users experience the service is a process map, not a journey map.

---

## Service Blueprint

**Definition:** A diagram that maps the full service delivery system, showing the relationship between the user-facing experience (frontstage), the supporting processes the user does not see (backstage), and the technical or organizational infrastructure (support processes). Service blueprints extend journey maps into the operational layer.

**Correct usage:** "The service blueprint shows that the 48-hour delay in claim response is caused by a manual handoff between two backend systems that requires a team lead's approval — a backstage bottleneck invisible to the customer."

**Common misuse:** Using "service blueprint" interchangeably with "journey map." A journey map shows the user's experience; a service blueprint shows how the organization delivers that experience. They are complementary but distinct tools.

---

## Convergence

**Definition:** A pattern or theme that appears independently across multiple participants, data sources, or research methods. Convergence strengthens confidence in a finding. The opposite — divergence — is equally important to report.

**Correct usage:** "We see convergence across interviews and diary entries: seven of nine participants described the same anxiety about response times, regardless of their demographic profile."

**Common misuse:** Claiming convergence when only two or three data points align, or ignoring divergent data to manufacture a cleaner narrative. True convergence requires multiple independent confirmations.

---

## Evidence Trail

**Definition:** The documented chain from raw data (observation or quote) through analysis (finding) to conclusion (insight or recommendation). An evidence trail makes the reasoning behind a conclusion transparent and auditable.

**Correct usage:** "The evidence trail for this recommendation starts with observations from P02, P05, and P08, converges into a finding about response-time anxiety, and leads to the insight that timeliness signals care — which is why we recommend proactive status updates."

**Common misuse:** Presenting recommendations without traceable evidence. Every insight and recommendation should be traceable back to specific research data. If the trail is missing, the conclusion is an opinion, not a research-backed finding.

---

## Confidence Level

**Definition:** An explicit assessment of how much trust should be placed in a finding or insight, based on the quality, quantity, and consistency of supporting evidence. Koos uses three levels: High (strong convergence, large or representative sample), Medium (moderate convergence, adequate sample), and Low (limited data, small sample, or mixed signals).

**Correct usage:** "Confidence: Medium. This finding is supported by consistent statements from 5 of 12 participants, but we have no observational or quantitative data to corroborate it."

**Common misuse:** Omitting confidence levels entirely, which leaves decision-makers unable to assess how much weight to give a finding. Also: inflating confidence to make findings appear more definitive than the data supports.

---

## Double Diamond

**Definition:** A design process framework consisting of four phases: Discover (divergent research), Define (convergent problem framing), Develop (divergent solution generation), and Deliver (convergent implementation). The two "diamonds" represent two cycles of expanding and then narrowing scope — first to find the right problem, then to find the right solution.

**Correct usage:** "We are currently in the Define phase of the first diamond — synthesizing our discovery research to articulate the core problem before we begin generating solutions."

**Common misuse:** Treating the Double Diamond as a rigid, linear sequence. In practice, projects move back and forth between phases. Also: skipping the first diamond (Discover/Define) and jumping directly to solution design without adequate problem definition.
