# Pains and Gains

## Origin and Purpose

The Pains and Gains framework draws from Alexander Osterwalder's Value Proposition Canvas, which maps customer pains and gains against the pain relievers and gain creators a product or service offers. At KOOS, we use pains and gains as a structured way to catalogue the negative experiences people encounter and the positive outcomes they seek or value. While JTBD captures what people are trying to achieve, pains and gains capture the texture of their current experience -- what frustrates, hinders, worries, delights, or exceeds expectations along the way.

Pains and gains are not abstract categories. They are specific, evidenced observations tied to real moments in a person's experience. They give design teams concrete material to work with: things to eliminate, reduce, create, or amplify.

## Pains: Three Categories

Pains describe the negative experiences, obstacles, risks, and undesired outcomes people encounter before, during, or after trying to get a job done.

**Functional pains** are things that do not work, take too long, or produce poor outcomes. They concern the practical, operational failures or frictions in an experience. Examples: a form that requires information the person does not have at hand; a process that requires visiting three different locations; a system that loses previously entered data when the session times out; inconsistent information across channels.

**Emotional pains** are the negative feelings the experience creates. These are subjective but very real, and they often have a stronger influence on behavior than functional problems. Examples: anxiety about whether an application was received; feeling judged during an intake interview; embarrassment at not understanding jargon used by a professional; helplessness when no one can explain next steps; the dread of calling a service line.

**Social pains** are the negative social consequences or perceptions the experience creates. Examples: appearing uninformed or unprepared in front of colleagues; being seen as a difficult or demanding customer; having to ask for help publicly; feeling that using a particular service carries stigma; being unable to explain to family members what is happening with a process.

## Gains: Four Levels

Gains describe the outcomes and benefits people want or would value. Not all gains carry equal weight. Osterwalder distinguishes four levels, which we find useful for prioritization:

**Required gains** are the baseline. Without these, the solution simply does not work, and people will not consider it functional. These are table stakes. Examples: a booking system that actually confirms the booking; a support line that is reachable during stated hours; a letter that arrives at the correct address.

**Expected gains** are things people assume they will get. They do not create delight, but their absence causes frustration. Examples: a professional who listens before offering advice; a website that works on a mobile phone; a receipt or confirmation after completing a transaction; reasonable waiting times.

**Desired gains** are outcomes people would love to have but do not necessarily expect. Delivering these creates genuine satisfaction and differentiation. Examples: a single point of contact who knows the full history; proactive updates before the person has to ask; being offered relevant options they had not considered; the ability to pause and resume a process without losing progress.

**Unexpected gains** are things people never thought to ask for but find delightful or deeply valuable when they encounter them. These are the hardest to identify through direct questioning because, by definition, people have not imagined them. They often emerge from close observation of workarounds and unmet needs. Examples: a service that automatically connects related requests the person filed separately; a professional who follows up weeks later to check how things went; receiving a clear plain-language summary after a complex meeting.

## When to Use Pains and Gains

Pains and gains analysis is applicable across multiple project activities:

- **Understanding current experience:** During the Discover phase, mapping pains and gains across the existing experience reveals where the greatest friction and value currently exist. This grounds the project in evidence rather than assumptions.
- **Concept testing:** When testing early concepts or prototypes, pains and gains provide evaluation criteria. Does the concept relieve a known pain? Does it create a desired gain? Does it inadvertently introduce new pains?
- **Value proposition design:** When defining or refining a service's value proposition, pains and gains are the raw material. A compelling value proposition addresses severe pains and creates meaningful gains for the people it serves.
- **Complementing JTBD:** Pains and gains work naturally alongside Jobs to Be Done. JTBD tells us what progress people are trying to make; pains and gains tell us what they encounter along the way. Together, they provide a complete picture of motivation and experience.

## How KOOS Applies Pains and Gains

**Always linked to specific touchpoints.** A pain or gain that floats in isolation is hard to act on. We tie each pain and gain to the moment, channel, or interaction where it occurs. "Anxiety about next steps" becomes actionable when we know it happens specifically after the initial assessment appointment, during the gap before a written report arrives.

**Always evidenced.** Every pain and gain in a KOOS deliverable should be traceable to transcript data, observation notes, or other primary evidence. We note the participant IDs and relevant quotes that support each finding.

**Severity matters.** Not all pains are equal. We assess and communicate relative severity -- a minor inconvenience is different from a barrier that causes people to abandon a process entirely. Similarly, gains vary in importance. We use participant language and behavioral evidence to gauge severity rather than imposing our own assumptions.

**Look for normalized pains.** Some of the most important pains are ones people have stopped noticing or complaining about because they have accepted them as inevitable. "That's just how it works" is often a signal that a significant pain has been normalized. These require careful listening and sometimes direct probing to surface.

**Gains can reveal unmet pains.** When someone expresses a desired gain, it often implies a current pain. "I wish someone would just tell me what to do next" implies that the current experience leaves people uncertain about next steps. Listening to gains with this lens doubles the insight available from each data point.
