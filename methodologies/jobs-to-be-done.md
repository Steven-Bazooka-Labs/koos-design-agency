# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

## Origin and Purpose

Jobs to Be Done is a framework originally articulated by Clayton Christensen and further developed by Bob Moesta, Tony Ulwick, and others. The core insight is deceptively simple: people don't buy products or use services for what they are -- they "hire" them to make progress in a specific circumstance. A job is the underlying progress a person is trying to make in a given situation. It is not a task, not a feature request, and not a demographic trait. It is the reason behind the reason.

At Koos, JTBD serves as a foundational lens for understanding human motivation. Rather than asking "what do users want?" we ask "what progress are people trying to make, and what circumstances shape that progress?" This reframing moves us from surface-level feature lists to the deeper motivational structures that drive behavior, decision-making, and satisfaction.

## The Three Dimensions of a Job

Every job has three intertwined dimensions. Isolating only one leads to incomplete understanding.

**Functional jobs** concern the practical progress someone is trying to make. These are the tangible, measurable outcomes: getting from A to B, completing a process, finding information, making a decision. Functional jobs are usually the easiest to identify because people can articulate them directly.

**Emotional jobs** concern how someone wants to feel (or avoid feeling) during and after the experience. These include confidence, peace of mind, a sense of control, reduced anxiety, or feeling competent. Emotional jobs are often more powerful drivers of behavior than functional ones, but they surface less readily in conversation. They require attentive listening and careful inference.

**Social jobs** concern how someone wants to be perceived by others -- or how they want to avoid being perceived. Looking professional, appearing responsible, being seen as a good parent, not seeming uninformed. Social jobs are shaped by the person's context: their role, their relationships, the norms of their environment.

A single hiring decision or service interaction typically involves all three dimensions simultaneously. A person choosing a healthcare provider is trying to get appropriate treatment (functional), feel safe and heard (emotional), and demonstrate responsible self-care to their family (social).

## The Koos JTBD Format

We express jobs using a structured sentence:

**"When [situation/trigger], I want to [motivation/desired progress], so I can [expected outcome/benefit]."**

Each component carries specific meaning:

- **When** captures the circumstance -- the situation, trigger, or context that creates the job. This is arguably the most important element. The same person has different jobs in different circumstances. A parent at a school meeting has different jobs than the same parent at a weekend outing. Context shapes everything.
- **I want to** captures the direction of desired progress -- what the person is trying to achieve or move toward. This should describe motivation, not a specific solution.
- **So I can** captures the expected outcome or downstream benefit -- why this progress matters to the person in their life or work.

Example: "When I receive a diagnosis I don't fully understand, I want to find trustworthy information that matches my specific situation, so I can feel prepared to ask the right questions at my next appointment."

## When to Use JTBD

JTBD analysis is valuable across the Koos project lifecycle, though its application shifts by phase:

- **Discover phase (exploratory research):** JTBD is a primary output of early research. During interviews and contextual inquiry, we listen for the jobs people are trying to do, the circumstances that create those jobs, and the solutions they currently hire (or fire). This forms the motivational foundation for all subsequent design work.
- **Develop phase (concept testing):** When evaluating concepts, we assess whether a proposed solution addresses real jobs. Does it help people make the progress they are seeking? Does it address functional, emotional, and social dimensions?
- **Deliver phase (evaluative):** In usability testing and service evaluation, JTBD provides the success criteria. We measure not just whether someone can complete a task, but whether the experience enables the progress they are trying to make.
- **Reframing exercises:** JTBD is particularly powerful for reframing problem statements. Instead of "what's wrong with the current service," we ask "what are people trying to achieve, and where does the current service fail to support that progress?"

## How Koos Applies JTBD

Several principles guide our use of JTBD:

**Every job must be backed by transcript evidence.** We do not invent jobs or infer them from assumptions. Each job statement should trace back to specific participant quotes and observed behaviors. This keeps our analysis grounded and defensible.

**Always consider the full situation.** The circumstance is not optional context -- it is the defining element. If you remove the "when," you lose the job. Two people in different situations may articulate similar desires but have fundamentally different jobs.

**Jobs exist in a hierarchy.** There are main jobs (the primary progress sought), related jobs (adjacent progress that matters), and smaller jobs that nest within larger ones. We map this hierarchy to understand which jobs are most important and how they relate.

**Distinguish main jobs from related jobs.** The main job is the core progress the person is hiring a solution to make. Related jobs are adjacent needs that arise in the same context. Both matter, but conflating them leads to unfocused design.

**Surface conflicting jobs.** People often hold jobs that are in tension with each other. They want to feel in control but also want someone else to handle the complexity. They want comprehensive information but also want simplicity. Identifying these tensions is where the richest design opportunities emerge.

## Common Pitfalls

**Confusing jobs with tasks.** "Fill out the form" is a task. "Ensure my application is complete and accurate so I don't face delays" is a job. Tasks describe actions within a system; jobs describe the progress that motivates those actions.

**Confusing jobs with solutions.** "I want a mobile app" is a solution. "When I'm away from my desk and need to check progress on a request, I want to get a quick status update so I can decide whether I need to follow up" is a job. Solutions are what get hired; jobs are the reason for the hiring.

**Writing jobs too vaguely.** "I want things to work well" captures nothing useful. A well-formed job is specific enough to inform design decisions. If two different solutions could equally satisfy the job statement, it is likely too vague.

**Ignoring the situation.** A job without a circumstance is an abstraction. "I want to feel confident" could mean anything. "When I'm presenting our team's recommendation to the executive committee" anchors the job in a concrete reality that designers can work with.

**Finding only functional jobs.** This is the most common failure mode, especially with less experienced researchers. If your job map has no emotional or social jobs, you have not listened carefully enough. The emotional and social dimensions are almost always present -- they simply require more attentive, empathetic inquiry to surface.
